A/B Testing Report

RSVP Experiment

We conducted two variations of A/B testing. In the first, we tested to see if asking visitors to RSVP on our homepage would increase event detail page views. The data is below:

The data shows very little difference between the variations. The page with RSVP links has a 53% chance of outperforming the original page: about what might be expected from a completely random sample from two identical pages. Over roughly 300 sessions, there is no discernible design conclusion; the RSVP text has little to no effect.

Donation Experiment

The second A/B test had us test whether asking users to “Please” donate would increase the number of donations for a given event. The data is below:

This round of A/B testing was more conclusive. Variation 1, which asked users to “Please Donate” clearly yielded more donation page views, with a 61.4% chance of outperforming the original, a statistically significant increase. In terms of design, this suggests that we should keep the “Please” on our donation links, and, more generally, that adding such pleasantries to our text can generate friendlier and more cooperative user traffic.

Referral Segmenting

In terms of user segmenting for the second A/B test, Google Analytics reported these segments for user data. (Note* Our own usage was cropped out of the image).

The skew towards yale.edu referrals is related to the link to our site that was posted on the Yale main page on February 16. Despite this massive skew—yale.edu had almost 19 times the referrals of the nearest competitor—its clickthrough rate stood at only 6.71%, giving it a minimal lead in terms of total clickthroughs—48.07%. law.yale.edu, on the other hand, had an impressive clickthrough rate of 85.39%, making up 32.62% total clickthrough completions, despite only being responsible for 4.58% of the traffic. While quite similar to law in terms of total referrals, som.yale.edu was relatively stingy in comparison, with a clickthrough rate of 35.71%, making up 12.88% of the total clickthroughs—typical business students. While only contributing 1.18% of total visits, divinity.yale.edu had a clickthrough rate of 43.48%, making up 4.29% of total clickthroughs. medicine.yale.edu had a minimal impact with 7 total pageviews and 1 clickthrough. search.yale.edu resulted in 10 pageviews and 0 clickthroughs. As a result of this experiment we have learned that, despite popular opinion, lawyers are, in fact, the best kind of people.

Retrospective

The past three weeks spent working on this project was a great learning experience on multiple levels. First, we went from mimicking agile scrum strategy to actually using it. During the first week, we did not do standup meetings properly and did not come up with a story log for the week beforehand. Rather, we met infrequently (basically during class) and added stories as we went. By the final week, we would meet everyday during lunch, at the very least via text, and were always up to date on the progress/trajectory of the other team members. Also, with the second week, we started to have a major meeting every Wednesday to decide on what the overall stories for the week were (deciding on a workload based on how many story points we handled the week before). Similarly, in the beginning we used git and version control only because we had to. As the weeks went on and we started to pick up on how to use it, we started to use git to efficiently work as a team. On top of all of this, we learned about a lot of cool and useful code and became much better at implementing it.

One thing to improve on in future projects would be coordinating so we can work together more often. We found that working in one place was the best way to get the most out of the work we were putting in. The benefits include being able to help each other when we are stuck and everyone learning at least a little about the code that we were individually working on.
